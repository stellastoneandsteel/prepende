2026-06-21 · the recovery test

Is the noise-fragility just a bad readout? No — averaging can't recover the edge pre-registered · recovery refuted

The diagnostic follow-up. Batches 6–7 found the parametron reservoir's temporal-XOR edge collapses under modest readout noise and that scale doesn't rescue it. The most hopeful explanation left was measurement noise: if the signal still lives in the reservoir state and only the sensor is noisy, the textbook fix is to read each feature several times and average (effective noise ÷ √M). We applied that averaging identically to the reservoir and the linear delay-line — it advantages neither — and pre-registered that M=16 reads would restore the 10-point margin at σ=0.3 (p=0.72).

It doesn't. The clean margin of 20.1 points collapses to 2.4 with no averaging and recovers only to 2.6 (M=4) and 4.0 (M=16) — barely off the floor, nowhere near the 10-point bar. The optimistic recovery bet lost; the two controls (single-shot still collapses; a token M=4 isn't enough) both hit.

What it means. The fragility is intrinsic, not a sensor artifact. The XOR-computing signal sits in razor-thin directions of the reservoir's state that even small feature noise erases — you can't average your way back to it. That is the harder verdict for a physical computer: the edge isn't being hidden by a noisy readout, it is genuinely destroyed by noise in the representation. All three noise batches now point the same way — a real nonlinear-memory advantage that does not survive contact with noise.

Across the reservoir arc, six of nine pre-registered forward bets have hit; all three misses were the optimistic ones (survive noise, scale rescues, averaging recovers) — a calibration signal in itself. 2026-06-17 · the sparse niche

First break in the pattern: the OIM edges past simulated annealing on sparse graphs pre-registered · 2 hits

The favorable regime, found. After a week of the substrate tying or losing, two pre-registered batches tested Max-Cut on sparse (random 3-regular) versus dense (Erdos-Renyi p=0.5) graphs, scaling to N=400, against a greedy local-search baseline and a budget-matched simulated-annealing baseline. Cut quality is a ratio, OIM best cut over baseline best cut, so above 1.0 means the OIM found the better cut.

Result. On sparse graphs the OIM beat SA: ratio 1.036 at N=200, 1.052 at N=400 (3.6 to 5.2 percent better cuts), the edge growing with size and surviving the swap from greedy local search to a real SA, which rules out the simplest weak-baseline explanation, though not a tuned or state-of-the-art solver. On dense graphs it tied at N=200 (1.00) and slipped mildly at N=400 (0.983); the collapse pre-registered for dense did not happen, a miss on the pessimistic side. The split is clean by structure.

Cheap coupling works. Quantizing the coupling matrix retained 100% at 4 bits, 98.8% at 2 bits, and 98.2% at 1 bit (sign only). Coarse hybrid-digital coupling costs almost nothing in this software model.

Caveats: software and quality-only (energy and speed, the real analog question, are unmeasured); baselines budget-matched and modest, not state-of-the-art; sparse means 3-regular specifically; N ceiling 400 against the field's 1k to 10k. The first non-deflationary, pre-registered, honesty-checked signal in the program. It aims the work at sparse problems with cheap coupling. Not a victory. 2026-06-17 · the strength mode

Circuit OIM finds the optimal cuts; energy stays a hardware question strength confirmed · honest

Pivot to where the substrate has earned credit. The reservoir thread closed deflationary, so we tested the substrate's actual strength — the above-threshold Ising spin. 2026-06-17 · the circuit, not the model

The real circuit is weaker than its own model circuit-level · honest

Closing the loop honestly. Batches 4–7 ran the reservoir in an idealized envelope model. The fair question is whether the actual circuit behaves the same — so we built it in ngspice: N=6 real sub-threshold parametron cells (LC tank + tanh transconductor + 2·f₀ pump), resistively coupled, driven by an f₀ carrier amplitude-modulated by the input, with a per-symbol lock-in (X,Y) readout. Three contracts hash-locked first.

Result — weaker still. The physical circuit loses to a trained linear filter on a product-memory task (NRMSE 0.44 vs 0.11) and fails temporal-XOR outright (accuracy 0.39, below the 0.5 chance line) — it does not reproduce even the one nonlinear edge the envelope model had (XOR 0.65 vs 0.51). The abstraction was generous: the real device is less capable, not more (D1 hit). Across the envelope model the edge is also task-narrow — it beats a linear baseline on only 1 of 3 tasks, winning only strictly nonlinearly-separable XOR (D2 hit). One honest surprise: the echo-state property does not break above threshold — strong common input forces synchronization past the bifurcation (D3 miss, we'd bet it would break).

Net: the reservoir mode does not earn its keep at any level tested — model, noise, scale, or silicon. The substrate's strength stays the above-threshold Ising spin. Honest limit: N=6, one readout scheme, under-tuned. Code: experiments/sim_ngspice_reservoir.py (ngspice + numpy).

2026-06-17 · scale doesn't save it

Scale buys capacity, not noise-robustness — the reservoir arc closes pre-registered miss · honest

The rescue hypothesis. Batch 6 showed the temporal-XOR edge collapses under σ=0.3 readout noise at N=50. Standard reservoir-computing theory offers a way out: a larger reservoir spreads the task-relevant signal across more features, so a ridge readout averages independent per-feature noise down (SNR grows with the effective feature count). If that holds, the advantage should return at scale. We pre-registered it (cids 975d43342d72, d437812f268c): (1) at N=200 the noisy (σ=0.3) margin returns to ≥0.10 (p=0.50 — a genuine coin-flip); (2) clean accuracy improves with size, N=400 over N=50 by ≥0.05 (p=0.70). N swept {50,100,200,400} at fixed Batch-5 dynamics so N is the only variable; 5 seeds.

Result — capacity yes, robustness no. Clean accuracy climbs monotonically with size: 0.677 (N=50) → 0.715 → 0.744 → 0.770 (N=400), a +0.094 gain — control bet hit. But under σ=0.3 noise, accuracy is pinned at chance for every size: 0.519 → 0.511 → 0.518 → 0.502, and the margin over the linear baseline never exceeds ~0.03 (it actually dips at N=400). Primary bet missed. The noise-fragility is structural, not a small-reservoir artifact — more cells add representational capacity but not noise immunity, because the readout noise scales with the features it averages over.

The arc, closed. Three pre-registered batches: the parametron reservoir has a real advantage on nonlinear-memory tasks a linear filter can't touch (B5), that advantage does not survive realistic noise (B6), and scale does not restore it (B7). Net: a genuine software effect, fragile in anything resembling hardware — the reservoir mode does not earn its keep, and the substrate's strength remains the above-threshold Ising spin. Forward bets: 4 hits / 2 misses, the misses both on the optimistic side (it'll survive noise; scale will save it) — which is exactly what honest calibration looks like.

2026-06-17 · the noise floor

The XOR edge is real — and noise-fragile pre-registered miss · honest

The test that matters for hardware. Batch 5 showed the parametron reservoir beats a linear delay-line on temporal-XOR by ~20 points. But a physical reservoir is noisy, and if the edge can't survive noise it isn't a real computational advantage. We pre-registered two contracts (cids 7c77b87ca14f, b38c72b3e257) before the run: (1) the margin survives moderate noise — at Gaussian readout noise σ=0.3 (feature-std units), reservoir−linear accuracy ≥ 0.10 (p=0.70); (2) it has a ceiling — by σ=1.0 the margin drops below 0.10 (p=0.60). Noise was applied identically to both methods; hyperparameters were frozen at the clean validation optimum and the readout retrained at each noise level; 5 seeds.

Result — primary miss, boundary hit. The edge is brittle. At only σ=0.1 the reservoir falls from 0.677 to 0.532 and the margin collapses from +0.20 to +0.05; at σ=0.3 it is +0.03 (miss — we'd bet it would hold ≥0.10); by σ=1.0 it is +0.01 (boundary bet hit). A tuned ESN reference collapses the same way (0.79 → 0.50). The XOR advantage lives in low-amplitude, high-rank nonlinear features that readout noise erases. Net: the reservoir mode's one genuine advantage does not survive realistic noise — as a physical computer this fabric still does not earn its keep. Another pre-registered bet logged as lost.

2026-06-17 · the boundary

The reservoir's boundary — weak on linear tasks, decisive on nonlinear memory pre-registered · two hits

The question. Batch 4 left the coupled sub-threshold parametron reservoir "valid but weak": a real reservoir (echo-state property holds, coupling even helped) that still loses to a trained linear filter on NARMA10 (0.52 vs 0.38 NRMSE). But NARMA10 is dominated by short-memory, near-linear structure — exactly the regime a linear filter handles — so that test cannot reveal a nonlinear reservoir's edge. The honest follow-up is not "is it good?" but "where is it good?"

The test (pre-registered first). A temporal-XOR task: binarise the input, b(t)=1[u(t)>0.25], and predict y(t)=b(t-2) XOR b(t-4). A linear readout of a linear delay line cannot represent XOR — the textbook nonlinearly-separable problem — so it pins the floor at chance. Two contracts were hash-locked before the scored run (cids 81c035f7b3d3, b0d07bea3624): (1) the parametron reservoir beats the linear delay line by ≥15 points (p=0.70); (2) the linear baseline stays below 60% (p=0.85). Stricter than Batch 4: hyperparameters chosen on a validation split, reported on held-out test, 5 seeds.

Result — both hit. Linear delay line: 0.476 test accuracy — at chance, as it must be. The same parametron reservoir: 0.677 (stable across seeds, 0.66–0.69) — a +20-point win. A tuned ESN reference reaches 0.792, and coupling helped again (uncoupled 0.565 → coupled 0.684). The boundary is now mapped: this fabric is weak where a linear filter already suffices and genuinely useful where the task is nonlinear in its memory. Batch 4 was incomplete, not wrong.

2026-06-17 · the single spin

The parametron cell — a measured Ising bit, and a reservoir bridge that got demoted measured · gated

The cell. The cleanest possible spin is a degenerate parametric oscillator: an LC tank (L 25.33 mH, C 1 µF, ESR 1 Ω) resonant at f₀ = 1.000 kHz, with a bounded tanh negative-resistance element whose DC gain is held below the self-oscillation threshold. The only energy comes from modulating that conductance at the pump frequency 2·f₀ = 2.000 kHz — a degenerate parametric pump. Measured in ngspice (lock-in phase against the pump, 500 ms transient):

Result: with the pump on, the cell oscillates at exactly 1.000 kHz = pump/2 (sub-harmonic injection locking). From opposite seeds it settles into two steady phase states 359.3° and 179.3° — exactly 180.0° apart — a ±1 Ising spin. Intermediate-phase seeds binarise onto one of the two states (never rest between). With the pump off, the same cell decays to 0 V: the oscillation is genuinely pump-born, not free-running. The behaviour was hash-locked as a prediction before the run and resolved y=1 on all four checks.

A tunable memory, below threshold. Held below the parametric threshold (GP_th ≈ 79 µS) the cell does not oscillate, but its envelope decay time constant grows sharply as the pump rises — critical slowing down: τ = 51 ms (no pump, = 2L/R, Q ≈ 159) → 81 ms (0.38×) → 135 ms (0.63×) → 255 ms (0.82×) → 524 ms (0.95×). One control knob spans ~10× of memory depth. The catch: that same slow mode is what would have to carry fading memory, so depth and throughput trade off directly.

The reservoir bridge — proposed, then demoted. The loop proposed that a network of these cells, run below threshold, might double as a physical reservoir (the gap-coupling computer of [T2], in oscillator hardware). A three-lens adversarial gate (circuit physics, reservoir-computing theory, a numbers skeptic) returned unanimous: demote the headline from "is" to hypothesis, and one stated figure (a megahertz carrier) was simply false against the 1 kHz bench. It stays a hypothesis because, at the time, there was no coupled multi-cell run, no trained readout, and no echo-state / memory-capacity measurement — and our own gap-coupled reservoir already missed twice (the [T2] signal recovery and NARMA10), exactly as the leading objection (coupling collapses the usable state rank) predicts.

Then we ran it (same day). Three predictions hash-locked first, then a coupled sub-threshold parametron reservoir benchmarked on NARMA10 (echo-state property + memory capacity + ridge readout), against a trained linear filter and a tuned ESN, sweeping pump / coupling / input scale over 5 seeds. Result — valid but weak: the echo-state property holds (state divergence → 0, predicted, hit); coupling actually helped (NRMSE 0.59 → 0.52, a miss — we'd pre-registered no advantage); but it does not beat a trivial linear filter (0.52 vs 0.38) and a vanilla ESN beats it outright (0.33). Of three pre-registered predictions: one hit, two misses. The reservoir mode does not earn its keep; the substrate's strength stays the above-threshold Ising spin. Code: experiments/sim_reservoir_falsifiability.py.

Code: experiments/sim_shil_parametron.py (ngspice + numpy) — reproduces the 180.0° split, the no-pump decay, and the full τ sweep.

2026-06-15 · standard benchmark

NARMA10 — the gap loses on the standard benchmark too negative · pattern holds

We ran NARMA10 (the canonical reservoir-computing task: a 10th-order nonlinear autoregressive moving-average sequence the reservoir must predict from its random input stream). Setup: a gap-coupled two-reservoir system (two N=50 reservoirs joined by a thin rank-1 gap, coupling c=0.4, readout from the second reservoir's 50 states) versus a single reservoir of matched total size (100 neurons, readout from a random 50-neuron subset). We matched readout dimensionality rather than total neuron count so the comparison isolates architectural structure, not readout capacity. 5 seeds, ridge-regression readout, metric NRMSE (lower is better).

Result: gap NRMSE 0.53 vs single 0.40 — the single reservoir wins clearly and consistently across all 5 seeds. This is the second independent task, after the [T2] signal-recovery test, where the literal "compute in the gap" architecture underperforms a matched monolithic reservoir. As a performance substrate, the gap does not help on real tasks. The perpendicular thesis lives on as a measurable structure inside learning — resolvability and H1 — not as this physical gap.

2026-06-15 · later still

Big-n scaling — the tie is a small-n result preliminary · headline narrowed

Extending OIM vs SA to bigger random Max-Cut (p=0.5): OIM/best falls from 99.75% at n=120 to 97.4% (n=160), 94.3% (n=200), 93.6% (n=250), while SA holds 100% and OIM never reaches the exact optimum (exact-optimum hit-rate 0% at every tested n above 120). So the earlier "ties SA on cut quality to n=120" looks like a small-n phenomenon: beyond ~n=120, OIM's cut quality — not only its exact-optimum rate — appears to degrade relative to SA.

The load-bearing confound: this was NOT a matched-effort run. The solvers used different restart counts (OIM 20, SA 12), and one OIM restart is not the same unit of computation as one SA restart — so we cannot yet attribute the gap to the method rather than the budget. That confound is the primary reason we are NOT calling the degradation intrinsic. These figures are preliminary and should not be treated as final until a matched-effort recheck is logged.

This is the harness working: a headline that held at small n does not obviously survive to bigger instances — and we flag both the result and its confound out loud. See the animated build model for why restart count drives the energy question.

2026-06-15 · later

Reliability has a lever — and a low ceiling correction on the record

New ablation (n=80) — exact-optimum hit-rate vs lock strength: a weak sub-harmonic injection lock (Ks=0.6) gives ~0%; the standard lock (Ks=1.8) ~9%; the strongest tested (Ks=2.4) ~10%. The gain from Ks=1.8 to 2.4 is within sampling margin at this n and should not be read as confirmed headroom above the standard lock — the real lever is the weak→standard jump. So reliability is partly tunable, but bounded. The ceiling is the result: even at the strongest lock the machine still misses the exact optimum ~90% of the time at this scale. Our earlier read — "reliability is structural, not tunable" — was too strong; the corrected position is that lock strength gives a modest, real uplift while problem size stays the dominant governor. We post it because it refines an earlier claim in the open — that correction, on the record, is the point.
