A physical reservoir's real edge — and the noise that takes it back

Three pre-registered batches map exactly where a coupled sub-threshold parametron fabric earns its keep as a computer, and exactly where it stops.

2026-06-17 · hash-locked predictions · numpy · five seeds throughout

The short version. A network of degenerate parametric oscillators, held below threshold, has a genuine computational advantage: on a task that is nonlinear in its memory — one a linear filter structurally cannot solve — it beats that filter by twenty points. But the advantage is fragile. Ten percent readout noise erases it, and making the reservoir eight times larger does not bring it back. As a piece of software it works; as a model of hardware it does not earn its keep. Every step below was a falsifiable prediction locked into a content hash before the run that resolved it — including the two we lost.

Why bother — and why pre-register

An earlier build proposed that the same oscillator fabric used as an Ising machine might double as a physical reservoir — a fixed nonlinear dynamical system you read out with a trained linear layer. An adversarial gate demoted the claim to a hypothesis. Then we tested it: on NARMA10, the standard reservoir benchmark, the parametron reservoir was valid but weak — a real reservoir (the echo-state property held) that nonetheless lost to a trained linear filter (0.52 vs 0.38 NRMSE). A vanilla echo-state network beat it outright.

That verdict was honest but incomplete. NARMA10 is dominated by short-memory, near-linear structure — exactly the regime a linear filter handles — so it cannot reveal whether a nonlinear reservoir has any edge at all. The sharper question is not "is it good?" but "where is it good?" To answer it without fooling ourselves, every claim below was hash-locked before its scored run, with its resolution rule and evaluation regime fixed at lock time. You cannot move the goalposts after the fact: editing any field breaks the hash, and the git commit of each lock is the external timestamp proving it preceded its outcome.

Batch 5 — the boundary: a task linearity can't touch

We chose temporal parity, the textbook nonlinearly-separable problem. Binarise the input, b(t)=1[u(t)>0.25], and ask the system to predict y(t)=b(t-2) XOR b(t-4). A linear readout of a linear delay line cannot represent XOR, so it pins the floor at chance. We pre-registered two contracts: the parametron reservoir beats the linear delay line by ≥15 points (p=0.70), and the linear baseline stays below 60% (p=0.85). Method was stricter than the NARMA10 run — hyperparameters chosen on a validation split, reported on held-out test.

Batch 6 — the noise wall

A real reservoir is analog and noisy, so the edge only matters if it survives noise. We pre-registered the test: add Gaussian readout noise (in feature-std units), identically to the reservoir and the linear baseline, and sweep it. Primary bet — the margin survives moderate noise, staying ≥0.10 at σ=0.3 (p=0.70). Paired bet — it has a ceiling, dropping below 0.10 by σ=1.0 (p=0.60).

Batch 7 — does scale rescue it?

Reservoir-computing theory offers a way out: a bigger reservoir spreads the task signal across more features, so the readout averages independent noise down. If true, the advantage returns at scale. We pre-registered it as a coin-flip (p=0.50) and swept the reservoir from 50 to 400 cells at fixed dynamics — alongside a control that bigger reservoirs are at least cleanly better (p=0.70).

The scorecard

Six forward predictions across the three batches, each locked before its run. Four hit, two missed — and both misses were on the optimistic side (the edge will survive noise; scale will save it). That is what honest calibration looks like: the bets we most wanted to win are the ones we lost.

What it means

The parametron reservoir has a real nonlinear-memory advantage that does not survive noise at any scale we tested. As software it works; as hardware it does not earn its keep.

This is a deflationary result, and that is the point. The honest finish is not a press release about a new kind of computer — it is a clean, falsifiable map of where an attractive idea actually holds (nonlinear tasks, noise-free) and where it breaks (any realistic noise, regardless of size). The substrate's genuine strength stays what it was measured to be earlier: the above-threshold Ising spin, not the sub-threshold reservoir. We will say so until a measurement says otherwise — and if one does, the ledger will show we changed our minds for a reason, on the record.
